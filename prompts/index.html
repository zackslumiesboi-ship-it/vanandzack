1The CTR Title Rewriter

Use when: a published page has impressions but a low click-through rate.

Here is my page title and meta description for a page targeting the query "[primary keyword]":

Title: [current title]
Meta: [current meta]

Rewrite the title 5 different ways to maximize CTR for this query. For each:
1. Front-load the keyword.
2. Add a specificity hook (number, year, comparison, or "vs").
3. Stay under 60 characters.

Then rate each one 1-10 on likely CTR vs the original, with a one-sentence why.

Why it works: forces specific tactics (front-loading, specificity hook) and forces self-evaluation. Generic "improve my title" gets you generic.

2The Meta Description That Earns the Click

Use when: same page, new meta description, ~155 characters.

For a page targeting "[primary keyword]" with this content summary:

[1-2 sentences of what the page actually delivers]

Write 3 meta descriptions that:
- Lead with the *outcome* the reader gets, not the topic.
- Include a soft objection-handler ("without [the painful thing]").
- Stay under 155 characters.
- Don't use the words "discover," "unlock," or "learn how."

Tell me which one is most likely to earn the click and why.

Why it works: bans the marketing-cliché vocabulary that signals AI slop and forces outcome-led framing.

3The FAQ Schema Generator

Use when: you want featured-snippet eligibility on an existing article.

Read this article: [paste article text]

Identify the 5 questions a real reader would type into Google after reading it (questions the article *almost* answers but doesn't fully).

For each, write:
- The question, phrased exactly as a person would type it (not a marketer).
- A 40-60 word answer drawn ONLY from the article. If the article doesn't fully answer it, say "article gap" — don't invent.

Output as a single JSON-LD FAQPage schema block I can paste into the page head.

Why it works: the "article gap" instruction is the trick — it surfaces content holes you can fill, instead of letting the model hallucinate answers.

4The Internal Link Suggester

Use when: you have a list of existing articles and a new draft.

Here is my new article draft:

[paste draft]

Here is a list of my existing published articles (URL — title — one-line summary):

[paste list]

Suggest internal links: 3-6 places in the new article where I should link to one of the existing articles. For each, give me:
- The exact sentence in the draft to add the link to.
- The URL to link to.
- The anchor text (3-6 words, descriptive, not "click here").
- Why this link helps the reader (not "for SEO" — for the reader).

Skip links that feel forced.

Why it works: "skip links that feel forced" is what stops the model from over-linking. The reader-justification keeps you honest.

5The Featured-Snippet Targeting Pass

Use when: you're writing a new piece and want a shot at position zero.

I'm writing a piece targeting the query "[query]". Here's my outline:

[paste outline]

For the H2 most likely to win the featured snippet on this query:
1. Tell me which H2 it is and why.
2. Rewrite that section's first paragraph as a 40-60 word direct answer to the query, structured how Google's snippet engine prefers (definition first, then 1-2 sentences of context).
3. Suggest a small list or table to add right after, since those compete for snippets too.

Why it works: most snippet content is bloated. The 40-60 word constraint and "definition first" structure mirror what actually wins snippets.

6The Content Brief Critic

Use when: someone (you, an agency, a freelancer) hands you a content brief.

Here is a content brief for a piece targeting "[query]":

[paste brief]

Be a skeptical senior SEO reviewing this brief. Tell me:
1. Is the search intent correctly identified? (informational, commercial, navigational, transactional — and what subtype)
2. Are the H2s in the right order for that intent, or are they in "what's easy to write" order?
3. What's the one thing this brief is missing that competitors ranking for this query have addressed?
4. Rate the brief 1-10. If under 7, rewrite the weakest H2.

Be direct. Don't soften.

Why it works: "be direct, don't soften" is doing real work — the model defaults to flattering review. Naming the role ("skeptical senior SEO") gets you better critique.

7The Cannibalization Detector

Use when: you suspect two pages on your site are competing for the same query.

Here are two pages from my site:

PAGE A:
URL: [url-a]
Title: [title]
Meta: [meta]
First 300 words: [excerpt]

PAGE B:
URL: [url-b]
Title: [title]
Meta: [meta]
First 300 words: [excerpt]

Are these pages cannibalizing each other on Google? If yes:
1. Which one should be the canonical winner and why (specifically what makes it stronger).
2. What should happen to the loser: redirect, consolidate, or rescope to a different query? Recommend one.
3. If "rescope," suggest the new query and a fresh angle.

Why it works: forces a recommendation, not just analysis. The "specifically what makes it stronger" prevents vague "page A is better."

8The Helpful-Content Audit Pass

Use when: a page is underperforming and you suspect it's getting downranked by Google's helpful-content systems.

Read this page: [paste content]

Audit it against Google's stated "people-first content" criteria. For each of these 8 questions, score 1-5 and explain in one sentence:

1. Does it provide original analysis or just rehash what's already ranking?
2. Does it demonstrate first-hand expertise (not just summarized expertise)?
3. Will a reader feel they got their question answered fully?
4. Will a reader feel they need to search again to verify or fill gaps?
5. Is the title an accurate summary or a clickbait promise?
6. Is the page padded with content the reader didn't ask for (long intros, irrelevant context)?
7. Does it answer the *next* question a reader would have, not just the headline one?
8. Would a domain expert in this field find anything wrong or thin in it?

Then give me the 2 highest-leverage fixes.

Why it works: these are Google's own questions from their helpful-content guidance. The "highest-leverage fixes" instruction stops you from drowning in 8 small to-dos.

9The Reader's First-Question Test

Use when: editing a draft. This is the single highest-ROI prompt in the list.

Here's my draft for a piece targeting "[query]":

[paste draft]

Imagine the reader who just typed [query] into Google and clicked your result. They're skimming the first 200 words.

1. What is the FIRST question their brain is asking as they land?
2. Where in the draft does that question get answered?
3. If it's not in the first 200 words: rewrite the opening so it IS.

Do this without adding fluff or padding.

Why it works: this is the entire game of on-page SEO and most drafts fail it. The constraint "without adding fluff" is what stops the model from "improving" your intro into corporate slop.

10The Competitor Gap Question

Use when: you're outranked and want to know what the top results have that you don't.

Here are summaries of the top 3 ranking pages for the query "[query]":

PAGE 1 ([domain]): [200-word summary]
PAGE 2 ([domain]): [200-word summary]
PAGE 3 ([domain]): [200-word summary]

Here's mine, currently ranking position [N]: [200-word summary]

Identify:
1. The one topic ALL three competitors cover that I don't. (Just one — the most important.)
2. The one structural element (table, comparison, calculator, original data, expert quote) that 2+ of them have and I don't.
3. The one *thing I do better* that I should make more prominent on the page.

Don't give me a list of 12 things. Just the highest-leverage gap and what's already working.

Why it works: the "just one" constraint prevents the audit-paralysis death-spiral. Knowing your own strength is just as important as knowing competitors'.
